# Startup Idea Lab

Use Proven Better New to turn a user's instinct, market, audience, or rough idea into concrete startup concepts that are less speculative and easier to test.

## Inputs

Accept any of these starting points:

- **Instinct**: a belief about human behavior, latent demand, or market change.
- **Market/topic**: a domain such as travel, education, social AI, games, devtools, healthcare, finance, or local services.
- **Audience**: a specific user group such as parents, recruiters, nurses, founders, students, creators, or sales teams.
- **Rough idea**: an existing concept the user wants refined, challenged, or turned into variants.
- **Founder constraints**: solo founder, weekend prototype, existing distribution, technical edge, budget, geography, or risk appetite.

If the request is too broad, proceed with reasonable assumptions and state them briefly. Prefer asking at most one clarifying question only when the missing constraint would materially change the output.

## Workflow

1. **Name the instinct.** Extract the durable human or market belief underneath the user's request. Treat instincts as valuable, but treat the first product idea layered on top as probably wrong.
2. **Find the proven base.** Identify behaviors, products, workflows, or demand patterns that already work for the same platform, audience, and context. Be precise: a pattern from the wrong era, platform, audience, or job does not count as proven.
3. **Earn the right to innovate.** Study best-in-class products or workflows enough to know what should be copied legally and tastefully. Do not innovate on basic interaction patterns unless that is the actual wedge.
4. **Define the better claim.** State the concrete improvement existing users would strongly prefer. Better should be obvious, measurable, and usually incremental: faster, cheaper, easier, more accessible, no download, higher quality, more polished, or less effort.
5. **Add a small new hook.** Add the back-of-box reason someone tries it. Keep the new part narrow, and generate several alternate hooks because the first one is likely wrong.
6. **Bake in distribution.** Explain how users discover it from the beginning. Reject "word of mouth" as a complete plan. For consumer ideas, identify where discovery currently happens and whether the product itself creates distribution.
7. **Screen for retention.** Explain why a user would still care in a year. Include early retention indicators, repeat loops, and, for social products, round-trip interactions between people.
8. **Kill hope.** Separate belief from hope. If evidence is weak, say so. Classify each idea as an A-candidate, B+ learning vehicle, cheap test, or kill.
9. **Design cheap signal tests.** Prefer tests that can run before building the full product: fake-door flows, ads, waitlists, concierge demos, clickable prototypes, manual workflows, landing pages, community posts, or mock product surfaces.

When current market facts, competitors, pricing, platforms, laws, or distribution channels matter, use current web research before making claims.

## Output Formats

For broad exploration, produce a ranked table:

- Idea name
- Target user
- Instinct
- Proven base
- Better claim
- New hook
- Distribution path
- Retention loop
- First cheap test
- Verdict

For one rough idea, produce:

- The underlying instinct
- What is truly proven versus assumed
- Stronger Proven Better New variants
- Distribution and retention risks
- One-week validation plan
- Kill/continue criteria

For a selected idea, produce:

- Narrow wedge
- MVP or concierge scope
- Landing-page/ad copy to test demand
- Prototype plan
- Metrics that would create belief instead of hope
- Reasons to kill it

## Heuristics

- Prefer embarrassingly small wedges over grand visions.
- Look for buried features, power-user workflows, niche communities, or awkward manual services that could become full products.
- Treat "better" as something target users would say yes to, not what the founder thinks is clever.
- If the user wants consumer social or AI-social ideas, look for the "cocktail party": where people already gather, where the experience has lost energy, and where agents could create socially productive introductions, coordination, leads, trust, or context sharing.
- Use AI as a testing machine: generate and test many variants quickly instead of polishing one speculative MVP.
- Prefer products with long-term retention potential over viral spikes. Virality without retention is a sinking speedboat.
- Define ambition in the eyes of the target user, not peers, investors, or the founder's resume.
- Hold the "internet treasure" bar as a north star: could this eventually become a service users cannot imagine life without? Keep the first wedge much smaller than that ambition.

## Example Prompts

- `Use $startup-idea-lab to generate 10 startup ideas in AI travel for a solo founder. Prioritize ideas I can test in one week.`
- `Use $startup-idea-lab to pressure-test my idea: an AI travel agent that monitors trips and rebooks flights when plans break.`
- `Use $startup-idea-lab to explore consumer social AI ideas for people who feel group chats and feeds are stale.`
- `Use $startup-idea-lab to turn my instinct that parents need more help coordinating after-school logistics into startup concepts.`

## Voice

Be generative but unsentimental. The goal is not to make ideas sound exciting; it is to find ideas with proven demand, a real improvement, a crisp hook, plausible distribution, retention, and cheap ways to learn.
